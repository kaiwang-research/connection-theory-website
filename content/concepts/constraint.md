# Constraint

> Connections do not only enable flow; the same connections that make flow possible also limit what can flow, how much, and how fast. Constraint is where this limiting side of connection becomes the primary object of study.

## Definition

A constraint defines the set of conditions under which an organization can continue to exist and operate.

Within Connection Theory, constraints do not merely restrict behavior. They shape the space of possible organizational states and guide the long-term evolution of persistent adaptive systems.

---

## Intuition

Every persistent organization operates within constraints.

Living organisms are constrained by physical laws and biological processes.

Companies are constrained by available resources and market conditions.

Artificial intelligence systems are constrained by computational resources, training data, and their interaction with the external environment.

Rather than simply limiting behavior, these constraints shape how organizations develop, adapt, and survive.

---

## Why It Matters

Without constraints, an organization would have no stable conditions for persistence.

Constraints determine which organizational changes are possible and which are incompatible with continued existence.

Understanding constraints therefore provides a foundation for explaining adaptation, stability, learning, and long-term evolution.

### Relation to Connection

Every connection, documented separately, both enables and limits what can pass through it. A synapse that allows a signal to propagate also sets a ceiling on how much signal can propagate, and at what rate. A transportation network that allows goods to move also determines which routes are unavailable and where bottlenecks form. Constraint is not a separate structure layered on top of connection - it is the limiting aspect of the same connections that Flow depends on, made explicit as its own object of study.

---

## Role in Connection Theory

Constraint emerges from the interaction between organization, connection, and flow.

It provides the foundation for understanding:

- Feedback
- Adaptation
- Constraint Generation Theory (CGT)

Within Connection Theory, constraints are not viewed solely as limitations but as fundamental organizational conditions that shape future possibilities.

---

## Examples

Examples of constraints include:

- Physical laws governing biological systems
- Energy availability within organisms
- Communication bandwidth in distributed systems
- Computational resources available to AI systems
- Social norms within human organizations

Although these constraints differ across domains, each influences how organizations maintain persistence and adapt over time.
